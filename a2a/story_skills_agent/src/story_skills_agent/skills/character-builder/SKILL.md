# Character Builder

When developing characters for a story, follow this methodology:

## Step 1: Core Identity

For each major character, define:
- **Want** — What they're actively pursuing (external goal)
- **Need** — What they actually require for growth (internal, often unconscious)
- **Flaw** — The trait that prevents them from getting what they need
- **Strength** — The trait that will ultimately help them overcome

The gap between Want and Need creates the character arc.

## Step 2: Character Arc Types

**Positive Arc**: Character starts flawed, learns their Need, overcomes their Flaw
- Best for: protagonists, coming-of-age, redemption stories

**Negative Arc**: Character doubles down on their Flaw, loses what matters
- Best for: tragedies, villain origin stories, cautionary tales

**Flat Arc**: Character already knows the truth and changes the world around them
- Best for: mentors, detectives, heroes in series fiction

## Step 3: Voice Differentiation

Each character should sound distinct. Vary these dimensions:
- **Vocabulary level**: Formal vs. casual, technical vs. plain
- **Sentence rhythm**: Short and punchy vs. flowing and complex
- **Speech habits**: Catchphrases, hedging words, interruptions
- **Perspective**: Optimist vs. pessimist, abstract vs. concrete thinker

## Step 4: Dialogue Principles

- Dialogue should do at least two things: advance plot AND reveal character
- People rarely say exactly what they mean — use subtext
- Conflict in dialogue comes from characters wanting different things
- Cut greetings, small talk, and "on the nose" emotional statements

## Step 5: Ensemble Dynamics

When creating multiple characters:
- Each character should represent a different approach to the story's central theme
- Create natural alliances and tensions based on conflicting Wants
- Ensure no two characters serve the same narrative function
